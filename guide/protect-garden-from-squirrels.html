Field Bulletin № 03 · Arachis hypogaea Protection

Three Methods
for In-Ground Peanut Exclusion

½-inch galvanized hardware cloth · landscape pins · zero chemicals

Squirrels attack a peanut patch in two distinct phases: seed-digging in the first weeks, then pod-digging at maturity. Each method below corresponds to a phase, or a level of pressure.

Method 01

Tunnel Cloche

Use weeks 0–6 · seedling phase
cross-section · single-row tunnel
Cut strip: 36" wide × (row length + 12")
Bend over: a 5-gallon bucket or by hand
Pin spacing: every 12–18" along both skirts
End caps: cut squares, pin down — no open ends

Sits over the row like a half-pipe. Easiest to build, fastest to lift off when you need to weed. Works until plants outgrow the arch height — then move to Method 2.

Method 02

Ground Skirt

Use weeks 6+ · pod / maturity phase
plan view · per-plant skirt with stem slit
Cut squares: 24"–30" per plant, or a long strip for the row
Stem slit: edge → center, just wide enough to slide around stem
Pin spacing: every 8–12" around perimeter — flat to soil
Cover with: straw or pine needle mulch (hides the wire)

Squirrels go straight down at the base of the plant to reach pods. Make them dig 8–12" out from the stem and they almost universally give up. Weeds will grow through the mesh — fine, that's the trade-off.

Method 03

Full Cage

Use when pressure is heavy · season-long protection
enclosed cage · lid hinges at rear
Frame: 1×2 lumber, screwed at corners
Mesh: hardware cloth stapled to top + all four sides
Bottom: open — use a ground skirt (Method 2) around the base
Lid: hinged at back so you can flip it open to weed/water

Overkill for a casual patch — necessary if you have a known squirrel highway through the yard. The hinged lid is the difference between a cage you'll actually maintain and one you'll abandon by July.
