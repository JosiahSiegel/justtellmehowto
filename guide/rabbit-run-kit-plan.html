Field Bulletin № 04 · Oryctolagus Containment

Kit-Based Walk-In Run
for Two Free-Roaming Bunnies

Quictent 12 × 9 × 6.6 ft kit + ½" hardware-cloth upgrades · buried footer · hutch inside · deck-anchored, slope-ready

The kit hands you the frame, the walk-in height, and a weather cover — the bulk of the labor. But it ships with ~1" chicken-grade mesh and an open bottom, so the work that actually makes it rabbit-safe is yours: line the gaps with ½" cloth, dig-proof the floor, and anchor it. The hutch lives inside.

The one thing the kit's label gets wrong — its mesh is ~0.78–1", not ½". At that size a raccoon can reach a paw through and a small rabbit can squeeze out, so "predator proof" is true for chickens, not for rabbits as-shipped. The ½" lining below is not optional.
Plan 01

The Layout

Top-down · 12 × 9 ft kit footprint · hutch inside
kit footprint · hutch + deck on the high end · kit tarp covers the whole top
Footprint: the kit's 12 × 9 ft, ~6.6 ft tall — bigger than a from-scratch 8×8, plenty for two
Hutch: sits fully inside on the high/driest end; shelter only — the lined perimeter is the predator barrier
Cover: the kit's PE tarp shades and rains-off the whole top; the deck adds shade on the high end
Door: the kit's own walk-in door — line it with ½" cloth like the walls (see Plan 02)
Site: clear the weeds + toxic lilies first; keep clear of the utility door swing, dryer vent, and downspout runoff
Plan 02

Kit Wall + ½" Liner

Cross-section · the upgrade that makes it rabbit-safe
kit frame + 1" mesh · ½" hardware-cloth liner on the reach-in zone
Liner: ½" hardware cloth over the lower ~2.5–3 ft of every wall and the door (inside face), zip-tied/hog-ringed to the kit mesh every ~4"
Why lower only: that's the predator-reach + rabbit-squeeze zone; the covered top stops climbers, so the kit's 1" mesh is fine up high
Top: confirm there's wire mesh under the tarp; if it's tarp-only, add mesh across the top
Cover: the tarp gives full shade/rain but "sails" in wind — strap or weight it down
The liner is the whole point of going this route — without it you've bought a chicken run, not a rabbit run. Use the kit's own heavy zip ties plus stainless hog rings, pull every seam tight, and leave no gap a ½" rule can't pass.
Plan 03

The Dig Footer

Detail · the open bottom must be closed
L-footer tied to the kit's base rail · digging defeated both ways
Tie-in: run the footer cloth up a few inches and zip-tie it to the kit's bottom rail — no gap between footer and frame
Bury: 8–12" straight down — defeats rabbits digging out
Apron: 12–18" horizontal, just under the surface, extending outward — stops predators digging in
Pin it: 6" landscape staples every 8–12", then re-cover with soil/sod
No wire floor: don't make them stand on mesh — keep solid ground inside (sore hocks)
Plan 04

Setting the Kit on the Slope

Mild · 3–12 in drop · level the pad, don't shim a tilt
level the footprint first · then the kit sits true and the footer closes the edge
Level first: a rigid kit can't take deeper posts on one side — scrape the high side and fill/tamp the low side to a level pad before assembly
Check drop: string-level across the footprint; mild 3–12" is an easy cut-and-fill (more than that, reconsider the spot)
Anchor: auger/ground anchors or rebar stakes at every corner + along the base; bolt the deck side to the deck
Edge gap: any remaining gap under the frame on the low side gets closed by the footer/skirt cloth

With a from-scratch frame you'd level the top and let the floor tilt. A kit is the opposite — its geometry is fixed, so you level the ground to match the kit. A shallow cut-and-fill on a mild slope takes an hour with a shovel and a rake, and it leaves the kit sitting square with the door swinging true.

Plan 05

Hutch Inside the Run

Existing hutch · high/dry end · shelter only
Placement: fully inside the kit on the high/driest end; its own door just opens into the run
No collar: inside the lined perimeter, there's nothing to seal it to — it's pure shelter
Base: paver pad over ~2" gravel — keeps wood off damp dirt (no rot) and dig-proof; near grade at the high end
Air gap: rubber/plastic feet or a PT 2×4 skid; seal the base rails with a pet-safe exterior sealer, cured before they're back in
Roof: angle the hutch's own lid to shed away from the open run
Open bottom: if they stand on the pavers, cover with a mat/straw to prevent sore hocks

Build

Step-by-Step

Order of operations · read it all before starting
Before you start — wear heavy gloves the whole time; cut ½" hardware cloth slices skin. Level the ground before the kit goes up, and keep a ½" reference (a finger, a rule) handy to test every gap as you close it.
01Site prep & level the pad
  1. Clear the footprint plus ~2 ft around it; fully remove the toxic lilies and keep the rose bush out of the zone.
  2. String-level across the footprint to read the drop (high end by the deck, low end downhill). Confirm it's a mild 3–12".
  3. Scrape the high side down and fill/tamp the low side until you have a level pad — a kit can't take a tilt, so level the ground to it.
  4. Mark where the kit sits, hutch end toward the deck/high side.
cut the high side, fill the low side → level pad
02Assemble the kit
  1. Build the frame per the kit manual — the push-button poles go together tool-free.
  2. Attach the mesh panels and the tarp cover with the kit's zip ties; tension everything so panels don't sag.
  3. Hang and test the door; confirm the latch seats and the frame sits square on your level pad.
03Anchor it down
  1. Drive an auger anchor or rebar stake at every corner and a few along the base rails; tie the frame to them.
  2. Bolt the deck-side frame to the deck posts with the ⅜" × 4" lag screws.
  3. Strap or weight the tarp tight — these covers catch wind and lift, so secure the edges and add sandbags if exposed.
04Dig & install the footer
  1. Dig a perimeter trench ~8–12" deep tight against the kit's base rail, with an outward shelf ~12–18" wide a few inches down.
  2. Run ½" hardware cloth up a few inches and zip-tie/hog-ring it to the base rail, drop it down the trench (buried leg), then bend it outward along the shelf as the apron.
  3. Pin the apron flat with 6" landscape staples every 8–12".
05Line the lower walls + door
  1. Cut ½" hardware cloth into ~2.5–3 ft tall bands and run them along the inside of every wall, overlapping the footer tie-in at the bottom.
  2. Fasten to the kit mesh every ~4" with hog rings (and the heavy zip ties as backup); pull seams tight.
  3. Line the door the same way so the closed door has no gap or 1" opening either.
  4. Fold or cap every cut edge so nothing sharp sits at rabbit height.
½" cloth laid over the kit mesh, fastened every ~4"
06Close any low-side gap
  1. If the leveled pad still leaves a gap under the frame on the low side, cut a short skirt strip of ½" cloth.
  2. Lap it over the base rail and lower liner, carry it to the ground, and bend the bottom outward as footer; pin it.
07Verify the top
  1. Confirm there's wire mesh under the tarp. If the top is tarp-only, add a layer of ½" or 1" mesh across it and tie it to the frame.
  2. Re-check the tarp is strapped/weighted so wind can't peel it back.
08Hutch pad & placement
  1. On the high/dry end, level a ~2" gravel bed and set 4–6 pavers flat and level, a touch proud of grade.
  2. Seal the hutch's base rails and feet; let it fully cure and off-gas.
  3. Set the hutch on rubber feet or a PT 2×4 skid on the pavers; angle its lid to shed away from the open run.
  4. If it's open-bottom and they'll stand on the pavers, lay a mat or straw against sore hocks.
09Final safety check & move-in
  1. Walk the whole perimeter inside and out, pressing every panel and seam — anything that flexes open or shows a gap > ½" gets another ring or staple.
  2. Test the latch the way a raccoon would: paw-poke, nose, and lift.
  3. Confirm no sharp cut-mesh ends at rabbit height; fold or cap any you find.
  4. Add hides, tunnel, water, and the litter trays in their corners, then let the rabbits in and watch them through the first day.
Heat is the silent killer in NC — rabbits handle cold far better than heat and can suffer fatal heat stroke above ~80–85°F. The kit's tarp gives full shade over the whole run, which helps a lot; still site it where the deck adds afternoon shade, and in summer add a frozen water bottle or a ceramic tile. Shade and fresh water matter as much as the mesh.
