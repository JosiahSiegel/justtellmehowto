Field Bulletin № 04 · Oryctolagus Containment

Steel-Pen Walk-In Run
for Two Free-Roaming Bunnies

Producer's Pride 8 × 8.4 × 6.7 ft poultry pen + ½" hardware-cloth liner · added shade · buried footer · hutch inside · deck-anchored

This pen is a strong starting point — a steel frame, walk-in height, a real welded-wire roof (predators can't get in from above), and a 2-way locking latch. But its wall mesh is ~2", far too open for rabbits; it has no floor; and it has no shade. So your work: line the lower walls with ½" cloth, dig-proof the floor, and add shade. The hutch lives inside.

What the pen's label gets wrong for rabbits — the wall mesh is ~2" (1.83" × 1.96" at the bottom). A rabbit can push its head through that and get stuck or escape, and a raccoon can reach a paw through. "Predator resistant" is a chicken claim; for rabbits the ½" lining below is mandatory.
Plan 01

The Layout

Top-down · ~8 × 8.4 ft pen footprint · hutch inside
welded-wire roof covers the whole top · add shade over the sunny end · hutch + deck on the high end
Footprint: the pen's ~8 × 8.4 ft, ~6.7 ft tall walk-in — room enough for two
Roof: welded-wire and predator-tight as-shipped; it gives zero shade, so lay shade cloth over the open/sunny end
Hutch: fully inside on the high/driest end; shelter only — the lined perimeter is the predator barrier
Door: the pen's own door + 2-way locking latch (padlockable); line its lower half with ½" cloth like the walls
Site: clear the weeds + toxic lilies first; keep clear of the utility door swing, dryer vent, and downspout runoff
Plan 02

Pen Wall + ½" Liner

Cross-section · the upgrade that makes it rabbit-safe
pen frame + 2" welded wire · ½" liner on the reach-in zone · shade added on top
Liner: ½" hardware cloth over the lower ~3 ft of every wall and the lower door (inside face), zip-tied/hog-ringed to the pen mesh every ~4"
Why ~3 ft: the 2" mesh is big enough that a reared-up rabbit can reach it, so go a touch higher than you would over 1" mesh
Why lower only: that's the predator-reach + rabbit-squeeze zone; the welded-wire roof already stops climbers up high
Shade: the wire roof gives none — lay shade cloth or a tarp over the open/sunny half and strap it down
The liner is the whole point of going this route — without it you've bought a chicken pen, not a rabbit run. Use heavy zip ties plus stainless hog rings, pull every seam tight, and leave no gap a ½" rule can pass.
Plan 03

The Dig Footer

Detail · the open bottom must be closed
L-footer tied to the pen's base rail · digging defeated both ways
Tie-in: run the footer cloth up a few inches and zip-tie it to the pen's bottom rail — no gap between footer and frame
Bury: 8–12" straight down — defeats rabbits digging out
Apron: 12–18" horizontal, just under the surface, extending outward — stops predators digging in
Pin it: 6" landscape staples every 8–12", then re-cover with soil/sod
No wire floor: don't make them stand on mesh — keep solid ground inside (sore hocks)
Plan 04

Setting the Pen on the Slope

Mild · 3–12 in drop · level the pad, don't seat a tilt
level the footprint first · then the pen sits true and the footer closes the edge
Level first: a rigid clamped pen can't take deeper legs on one side — scrape the high side and fill/tamp the low side to a level pad before assembly
Check drop: string-level across the footprint; mild 3–12" is an easy cut-and-fill (more than that, reconsider the spot)
Anchor: auger/ground anchors or rebar stakes at every corner + along the base; bolt the deck side to the deck
Edge gap: any remaining gap under the frame on the low side gets closed by the footer/skirt cloth

With a from-scratch frame you'd level the top and let the floor tilt. A clamp-together pen is the opposite — its geometry is fixed, so you level the ground to match the pen. A shallow cut-and-fill on a mild slope takes an hour with a shovel and a rake, and it leaves the pen sitting square with the door swinging true.

Plan 05

Hutch Inside the Run

Existing hutch · high/dry end · shelter only
Placement: fully inside the pen on the high/driest end; its own door just opens into the run
No collar: inside the lined perimeter, there's nothing to seal it to — it's pure shelter
Base: paver pad over ~2" gravel — keeps wood off damp dirt (no rot) and dig-proof; near grade at the high end
Air gap: rubber/plastic feet or a PT 2×4 skid; seal the base rails with a pet-safe exterior sealer, cured before they're back in
Roof: angle the hutch's own lid to shed away from the open run — it's also their dry retreat under the open wire roof
Open bottom: if they stand on the pavers, cover with a mat/straw to prevent sore hocks

Build

Step-by-Step

Order of operations · read it all before starting
Before you start — wear heavy gloves the whole time; cut ½" hardware cloth slices skin. Level the ground before the pen goes up, and keep a ½" reference (a finger, a rule) handy to test every gap as you close it.
01Site prep & level the pad
  1. Clear the footprint plus ~2 ft around it; fully remove the toxic lilies and keep the rose bush out of the zone.
  2. String-level across the footprint to read the drop (high end by the deck, low end downhill). Confirm it's a mild 3–12".
  3. Scrape the high side down and fill/tamp the low side until you have a level pad — a clamped pen can't take a tilt, so level the ground to it.
  4. Mark where the pen sits, hutch end toward the deck/high side.
cut the high side, fill the low side → level pad
02Assemble the pen
  1. Connect the panels with the clamps per the pen manual; stand the walls and fit the welded-wire roof.
  2. Hang and test the door; confirm the 2-way latch seats and the frame sits square on your level pad.
  3. Snug every clamp — loose joints leave gaps and let the structure rack.
03Anchor it down
  1. Drive an auger anchor or rebar stake at every corner and a few along the base rails; tie the frame to them.
  2. Bolt the deck-side frame to the deck posts with the ⅜" × 4" lag screws.
  3. Add the padlock if you want the extra security — the latch is built for it.
04Dig & install the footer
  1. Dig a perimeter trench ~8–12" deep tight against the pen's base rail, with an outward shelf ~12–18" wide a few inches down.
  2. Run ½" hardware cloth up a few inches and zip-tie/hog-ring it to the base rail, drop it down the trench (buried leg), then bend it outward along the shelf as the apron.
  3. Pin the apron flat with 6" landscape staples every 8–12".
05Line the lower walls + door
  1. Cut ½" hardware cloth into ~3 ft tall bands and run them along the inside of every wall, overlapping the footer tie-in at the bottom.
  2. Fasten to the welded wire every ~4" with hog rings (and heavy zip ties as backup); pull seams tight.
  3. Line the lower half of the door the same way so the closed door has no 2" opening either.
  4. Fold or cap every cut edge so nothing sharp sits at rabbit height.
½" cloth laid over the 2" welded wire, fastened every ~4"
06Close any low-side gap
  1. If the leveled pad still leaves a gap under the frame on the low side, cut a short skirt strip of ½" cloth.
  2. Lap it over the base rail and lower liner, carry it to the ground, and bend the bottom outward as footer; pin it.
07Add shade
  1. Lay shade cloth or a tarp over the open/sunny half of the wire roof — the deck shades the hutch end, this covers the rest.
  2. Fasten it to the roof wire with bungee balls or zip ties, and weight/strap the edges so wind can't peel it back.

Tip: shade cloth breathes better than a solid tarp in summer heat; a tarp also adds rain cover if you want it.

08Hutch pad & placement
  1. On the high/dry end, level a ~2" gravel bed and set 4–6 pavers flat and level, a touch proud of grade.
  2. Seal the hutch's base rails and feet; let it fully cure and off-gas.
  3. Set the hutch on rubber feet or a PT 2×4 skid on the pavers; angle its lid to shed away from the open run.
  4. If it's open-bottom and they'll stand on the pavers, lay a mat or straw against sore hocks.
09Final safety check & move-in
  1. Walk the whole perimeter inside and out, pressing every panel and seam — anything that flexes open or shows a gap > ½" gets another ring or staple.
  2. Test the latch the way a raccoon would: paw-poke, nose, and lift.
  3. Confirm no sharp cut-mesh ends at rabbit height; fold or cap any you find.
  4. Add hides, tunnel, water, and the litter trays in their corners, then let the rabbits in and watch them through the first day.
Heat is the silent killer in NC — rabbits handle cold far better than heat and can suffer fatal heat stroke above ~80–85°F. This pen's wire roof gives no shade on its own, so the shade cloth in step 07 isn't optional in summer; site it where the deck adds afternoon shade too, and add a frozen water bottle or a ceramic tile on hot days. Shade and fresh water matter as much as the mesh.
