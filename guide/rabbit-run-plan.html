Field Bulletin № 04 · Oryctolagus Containment

A Walk-In Predator-Proof Run
for Two Free-Roaming Bunnies

8 × 8 ft · ~6½ ft walk-in · ½" hardware cloth · covered top · buried footer · hutch inside · deck-attached, slope-ready

A walk-in run keeps rabbits from digging out, keeps predators from getting in, keeps the heat off — and lets you step inside to clean and visit. The hutch lives inside the fence, so the run's perimeter is the only barrier that has to be predator-tight.

Plan 01

The Layout

Top-down · 8 × 8 ft = 64 sq ft · hutch inside
deck shades + anchors the high end (left) where the hutch sits; shade panel over the open sunny end (right); walk-in door at the front
Footprint: 8 × 8 ft, ~6½ ft tall — roomy walk-in for two rabbits
Hutch: sits fully inside the run on the high/driest end; it's shelter only — the run perimeter is the predator barrier, so no collar or mesh-lining needed
Hutch base: paver pad over gravel — keeps wood off damp dirt (no rot) and dig-proof; near grade at the high end, so no wedge to fill here
Access: a framed walk-in door (~30" × 6 ft) on the front wall with a raccoon-proof latch
Shade: the deck shades the high/hutch end; the fixed panel covers the open sunny end — full coverage
Inside: 2+ hides, a tunnel, water out in the open run
Site: clear the weeds + toxic lilies first; keep clear of the utility door swing, dryer vent, and downspout runoff
Plan 02

Walls, Covered Top & Door

Cross-section · tall enough to stand inside
walk-in height · full mesh roof + shade panel on top · door in one wall
Height: ~6½ ft clear inside (posts ~8 ft, set ~1½ ft in the ground)
Mesh: ½" galvanized hardware cloth — NOT chicken wire (see note)
Roof: fully meshed top with the shade panel laid on top of part — no climber gap
Door: a framed ~30" × 6 ft door, mesh-skinned, 3 hinges + a raccoon-proof two-step latch
Frame: 4×4 corner posts; 2×4 plates, mid-rails, studs; 2×4 roof joists
Why not chicken wire — its 1" hexagons let raccoons reach through and tear under a fox. ½" hardware cloth is the only mesh that holds. To save cost on a tall wall you may run ½" hardware cloth on the bottom ~4 ft (the reach-in zone) and 1" welded wire above, but the roof and that lower band must be hardware cloth, and the whole top must be covered — that's what stops hawks, owls, raccoons, and cats.
Plan 03

The Dig Footer

Detail · the single most-skipped step
L-footer · belt-and-suspenders against digging both ways
Bury wall: 8–12" straight down — defeats rabbits digging out
Apron: 12–18" horizontal, just under the surface, extending outward
Pin it: landscape staples every 8–12", then re-cover with soil/sod
No wire floor: standing on mesh causes sore hocks — keep solid ground inside
Plan 04

Building on the Slope

Mild · 3–12 in drop · hutch on the high end
level top frame · hutch at the dry high end · skirt closes the low-end wedge
Frame: build the top frame dead level; set posts deeper on the high side so the top stays horizontal
Walls: uniform ~6½ ft of mesh all around
Skirt strip: cloth along the downhill edges, lapped to the ground, bottom 12" bent outward as footer
Hutch end: at the dry high end the hutch sits near grade — little to no wedge, so its pad just keeps wood off the dirt
Anchor: deck side bolts to the deck posts; set the other posts in concrete; gravel the downhill edge

On a mild slope you don't terrace — you build the top dead level and let the floor stay sloped. The only wedge gap is along the low (downhill) end, and with the hutch now at the opposite high end, that gap is simply closed by the skirt strip with nothing in the way. The hutch sits at the dry high end where there's little to no wedge, so its pad only has to keep wood off the damp ground.

Plan 05

Hutch Inside the Run

Existing hutch · high/dry end · shelter only
Placement: fully inside the run on the high/driest end; its own door just opens into the run
No collar: because it's inside the predator-tight perimeter, you don't need to seal it to a run wall or line its welded-wire sides
Base: paver pad over ~2" gravel — keeps wood off damp dirt (no rot) and dig-proof; near grade at the high end, so no wedge to fill
Air gap: rubber/plastic feet or a PT 2×4 skid between hutch and pavers; seal the base rails with a pet-safe exterior sealer, cured before they're back in
Drainage: set the pad a touch proud of grade; being the high end, it sheds naturally — no swale needed here
Roof: angle the hutch's own lid to shed away from the open run
Open bottom: if they stand on the pavers, cover with a mat/straw to prevent sore hocks

Moving the hutch inside the fence removes the trickiest detail from the earlier plan — there's no longer a hutch-to-run seam to predator-proof. The hutch becomes pure shelter; the walk-in run's perimeter does all the defending. Putting it at the high end is the right call: it's the driest spot, so the pad mostly just keeps wood off the ground, and the slope's wedge is dealt with separately at the far low end.

Materials

Bill of Materials

8 × 8 × ~6½ ft walk-in · dirt floor · deck-attached
Lumber & framing
4 × 4×4×8 ft pressure-treated — corner posts (set in concrete)
~26 × 2×4×8 ft — top plates, bottom sills, mid-rails, wall studs, door frame, roof joists

Rough breakdown: 4 top plates + 4 bottom sills + 4 mid-rails + ~8 wall studs + ~3 for the door frame + ~3 roof joists. Exact count depends on stud spacing (~24–32") and door size. Set posts ~1½ ft deep so ~6½ ft stands clear.

Hardware cloth & mesh — ½" galvanized
2 × 100 ft × 48" ½" hardware cloth rolls — full-height walls + footer + roof + door (max safety)
— or — 1× 100 ft ½" cloth (lower ~4 ft + footer + roof) + 1× 50 ft 1" welded wire (upper walls) to save cost

The walls are now ~6½ ft tall, so wall mesh more than doubles vs. the old 3-ft box (~32 ft perimeter × ~6½ ft + footer + 64 sq ft roof ≈ 130 linear ft of 48" cloth). The lower ~4 ft and the whole roof must be ½" hardware cloth; upper walls can be 1" welded wire since the covered top stops climbers.

Roof shade panel
2 × ~26" × 8 ft corrugated polycarbonate (or one 4×8 ply) — over the low/sunny end, on top of the mesh
Door & post setting
1 set 3 exterior hinges + raccoon-proof two-step latch (or barrel bolt + carabiner)
4 bags fast-setting concrete — set the non-deck posts (the 2 deck-side posts bolt to the deck instead)
4 × ⅜" × 4" lag screws + washers — bolt the deck-side wall to the deck posts
Hutch base (inside, high end)
4–6 × concrete pavers (or large stepping stones) — raised pad under the hutch
~3 cu ft gravel — pad bed + downhill-edge drainage
1 × pet-safe exterior wood sealer — hutch base rails & feet
4 × rubber/plastic feet (or 1 PT 2×4×8 for skids) — air gap under the hutch
Fasteners & hardware
1 lb 3" exterior deck screws — frame joints
1 box structural/exterior screws or post brackets — 4×4 connections
2 lb poultry/fence staples + fender washers — cloth to wood (more wall area now)
1 pack stainless hog rings (+ pliers) or zip ties — cloth-to-cloth seams
50 pk 6" landscape staples — pin the footer + skirt
Tools
— drill/driver, post-hole digger, aviation snips/cloth cutters, heavy gloves, tape, square, level, hammer/staple gun, socket or wrench, safety glasses

A walk-in roughly doubles the old $200–300 estimate — figure ~$400–600, the hardware-cloth and posts/concrete being the biggest swings. Prices move and vary by region; treat it as a ballpark, not a quote.

Build

Step-by-Step Construction

Order of operations · read it all before starting
Before you start — wear heavy gloves the whole time; cut ½" hardware cloth edges slice skin. Dry-fit lumber with a couple of screws before driving everything home, and keep checking square (equal diagonals) and level as you go. Fold or cap every cut mesh end so nothing sharp sits at rabbit height.
01Site prep & layout
  1. Clear the 8×8 footprint plus ~2 ft of working room around it — pull weeds and grass, and fully remove the toxic lilies; keep the thorny rose bush outside the build zone.
  2. String-level across the 8 ft to confirm the drop: high end by the deck/crawlspace door, low end downhill. Confirm it's in the mild 3–12" range (if it's more, you terrace instead — stop and re-plan).
  3. Mark the 8×8 square with stakes and string. Square it with the 3-4-5 method (6 ft on one side, 8 ft on the other, diagonal reads 10 ft), then confirm both diagonals are equal.
  4. Mark the four corner-post spots at the string intersections; note the two that sit against the deck.
square check · equal diagonals (or the 3-4-5 rule)
02Set the posts
  1. Dig the two non-deck corner holes ~10–12" wide × ~18–24" deep (add mid-span post holes if a wall feels flimsy at 8 ft).
  2. Stand a 4×4 in each hole. Position the two deck-side 4×4s against the deck framing — those get bolted, not dug.
  3. Set a level top across all posts: on the slope, the high-side posts sit deeper so every post top lands at the same height (~6½ ft above the high-side grade). Use a line level or laser.
  4. Plumb each post on two faces, brace with scrap, then pour fast-setting concrete in the dug holes. Bolt each deck-side post to the deck with two ⅜" × 4" lag screws + washers.
  5. Let the concrete cure (per the bag) before you hang any weight on the frame.

Tip: setting the top level — not the ground — is the whole trick on a slope. The floor staying tilted is fine.

03Frame the walls & roof structure
  1. Screw the four top plates (2×4×8) post-to-post around the top, dead level — this locks the box square.
  2. Add the four bottom sills near grade, also level (they float above grade on the low side — expected).
  3. Add one mid-rail per wall at about mid-height for mesh backing and rigidity.
  4. Add vertical studs between plates every ~24–32" (roughly two per wall) to back the mesh.
  5. Frame the door's rough opening in a front-wall bay: two king studs with a header at ~6 ft, leaving a ~30" wide × ~6 ft opening.
  6. Lay the three roof joists across the 8 ft top, splitting it into bays. Use structural screws or post/beam brackets at the 4×4 connections.
one framed wall · door bay on the right
04Dig the footer trench
  1. Around the full outside perimeter, dig a trench ~8–12" deep tight against the sills.
  2. Cut an outward shelf ~12–18" wide and a few inches deep off the bottom of that trench — this is where the apron lies flat.
  3. Where the run meets the deck, keep the footer tight to the deck base with no gap wider than ½".
05Hang the wall mesh
  1. Work the ½" hardware cloth along the inside face of the frame, in horizontal bands, starting at a corner.
  2. Lower band (48" cloth): ~36" covers the wall while the bottom ~12" drops into the trench as the buried leg, then bends outward along the shelf as the 12–18" apron.
  3. Staple to every framing member every ~4" with poultry staples; add fender washers at high-stress spots.
  4. Upper band: run the second course (½" cloth for max safety, or 1" welded wire to save cost) from the mid-rail to the top plate, overlapping the lower band ~2".
  5. Seam every cloth-to-cloth overlap — corners and band overlaps — with stainless hog rings or zip ties every ~4" so no gap exceeds ½".
  6. Pin the apron flat in its shelf with 6" landscape staples every 8–12".
two mesh bands overlap; bottom bends into the buried L
06Close the slope wedge
  1. On the downhill end, where the level sill floats above grade, cut a 24"-tall skirt strip of cloth.
  2. Lap it over the lower wall mesh and the sill, carry it down to the actual ground, and bend the bottom 12" outward as footer.
  3. Hog-ring the overlap every ~4" and pin the footer with landscape staples.
07Build & hang the door
  1. Build a 2×4 door frame to fit the opening with ~¼" clearance all around (≈29½" × ≈5'11").
  2. Skin it with ½" hardware cloth stapled to the inside face; fold or cap the cut edges.
  3. Hang it on three exterior hinges fixed to one king stud.
  4. Fit the raccoon-proof two-step latch (or barrel bolt + spring carabiner) on the latch side, plus a hook or turn-button low down so it can't be nosed open.
  5. Check it swings clear and closes with no gap wider than ½" anywhere around the frame.
door · hinges one side, raccoon-proof latch the other
08Mesh & cover the roof
  1. Mesh the entire top: lay ½" hardware cloth across all roof joists, overlap strips ~2", and staple to every joist every ~4".
  2. Hog-ring the roof mesh to the top-plate wall mesh all the way around — the roof-to-wall seam is a favorite climber entry, so leave zero gap.
  3. Lay the corrugated shade panels on top of the mesh over the sunny-end half; screw through the ridges into the joists with washered screws. Leave the other half open mesh for airflow.
  4. Give the panels a slight slope/overhang so rain sheds off the downhill side, away from the door.
roof-to-wall seam · hog-ring tight, then panel on top
09Backfill & drainage
  1. Backfill the footer trench over the apron with the excavated soil; tamp it and re-lay sod if you have it.
  2. Lay gravel along the downhill edge so the low corner sheds instead of pooling.
10Hutch pad & placement
  1. On the high/dry end, level a ~2" gravel bed and set 4–6 pavers flat and level, a touch proud of grade.
  2. Seal the hutch's base rails and feet with the pet-safe exterior sealer; let it fully cure and off-gas.
  3. Set the hutch on rubber feet or a PT 2×4 skid on the pavers for an air gap; angle its lid to shed away from the open run.
  4. If it's open-bottom and they'll stand on the pavers, lay a mat or straw so they don't get sore hocks.
11Final safety check & move-in
  1. Walk the whole perimeter inside and out, pressing on every panel and seam — anything that flexes open or shows a gap > ½" gets another staple or hog ring.
  2. Test the latch the way a raccoon would: paw-poke, nose, and lift.
  3. Confirm no sharp cut-mesh ends sit at rabbit height; fold or cap any you find.
  4. Add the hides, tunnel, water, and litter trays in their corners, then let the rabbits in and watch them through the first day.
Heat is the silent killer in NC — rabbits handle cold far better than heat and can suffer fatal heat stroke above ~80–85°F. The taller walk-in actually helps (more air volume), but still site it in afternoon shade — the deck shades the hutch end and the panel shades the open end — and in summer add a frozen water bottle or a ceramic tile. Shade and fresh water matter as much as the mesh.
